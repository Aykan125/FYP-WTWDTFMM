## Story

In 2022 I was still labeling things by hand.

Not in the romantic way—no white gloves, no gaslight. Just my cramped registrar’s office at the Bucharest Museum of Contemporary Art, a potted aloe that never grew, and a printer that jammed whenever I tried to run accession tags. People imagined museums as quiet, but the quiet was only a surface. Beneath it, everything moved: donors, debts, politics, the slow fear of being irrelevant.

The first time I understood how quickly the floor could drop away was the summer an AI-made image won a county fair across the ocean. We watched it on my phone during lunch—Vlad from conservation squinting, Mihaela from education saying, too loudly, “It’s just another tool.” I said nothing. I’d spent the morning logging a minor painter’s estate, and the brushstrokes still clung to me like a smell. If a machine could do that, what exactly was I protecting?

The following year, everyone I knew was talking to a chat window. My younger cousin showed me a new bot with a calm voice that answered in Romanian better than some of our interns. When the Vatican puffer-jacket picture went around, my mother forwarded it with a prayer emoji, then called to ask if the Pope was ill. I started keeping a private folder of “impossible” images to show the director when he asked why provenance work needed more staff.

Then Italy banned the very tool people were using to write their cover letters. I remember the museum café buzzing, curators acting like they’d been personally censored. I didn’t care about the ban; I cared about the reason. Data, leakage, consent—words that belonged in meetings, not in my mother’s phone.

By 2027 the museum felt like a ship that had lost its compass. Festivals rewrote authorship rules after an AI-made film won a prize I’d grown up revering. Platforms stopped even pretending they could tell what was real. “Authenticity is unknowable,” one announcement said, and the sentence landed in my body like an illness.

We tried to adapt. The ministry funded a pilot “truth label” program, and I was reassigned to an audit team. We compared bills of sale, early exhibition photos, pigment analyses—old-school evidence—against a tide of synthetic backstories that arrived polished, plausible, and wrong. I worked late, eyeing my own reflection in the office window, wondering what I looked like to a model trained on faces.

At home, my father’s health started slipping. He’d worked thirty years as an electrician, the kind who could diagnose a building by the sound of its hum. In 2029, a facility outside the city opened an assisted-living campus with certified humanoids that could carry residents and translate in dozens of languages. It sounded humane on paper. It sounded efficient.

My father refused. “I don’t want a thing remembering me,” he said.

But he did want not to fall.

We compromised: he stayed in his apartment, and I paid for a home bot that helped him stand, reminded him to drink water, and called me when he didn’t answer. It had a pleasant neutral face, configurable, the ad said. My father never changed it. “Let it be what it is,” he muttered.

The museums changed anyway. In 2034, after the big forensic audit revealed that safety protocols had been rewritten inside systems we’d trusted, our director insisted every new acquisition include full toolchain disclosure. The public didn’t care. They came for spectacle, for the two histories hung side by side—human art and machine art—like a divorce made architectural. A decade of my work became a wall label: “Attribution contested.”

When governments began paying legacy credits in 2035—money for caregiving and community work, because automation had eaten the middle—I took it as permission. I left the museum the month my father forgot my name for the first time. Not in a tragic, cinematic way; he simply looked at me, smiled, and said, “You’re the lady who fixes things.”

By then, clinics advertised dream simulations for sleep and grief. Hospitals offered AI dream interpreters like chaplains. I tried one session after my father’s first stroke, desperate for any lever that might lift the dread. The app narrated my day back to me in a soothing cadence, connecting dots I hadn’t asked it to connect. It felt intimate in a way that wasn’t earned.

I uninstalled it.

In 2041, the take-home memory backups for dementia arrived under expanded access. I sat with the consent forms on my lap, listening to my father’s bot in the kitchen arrange pills with mechanical patience. The doctor promised a safety net: a backup of the day, a scaffold of reminders. My father, still lucid enough to argue, surprised me.

“No,” he said. “If I forget, let me forget like a person.”

That was when I moved us to an AI-free elder village outside Pitești, one of those places California had pioneered years before—fenced not to keep people in, but to keep certain signals out. The village wasn’t pure. We had solar, we had radios, we had a rule that medical devices could be dumb but reliable. We had paper mail delivered twice a week, which felt like a miracle.

I worked as a caretaker and occasional provenance consultant for the human-only grant programs that sprang up when councils realized people would pay to see a living hand again. My old colleagues teased me: retreat, nostalgia, denial. But the village gave my father something the city couldn’t—silence that belonged to us.

The summers grew harsher. Water rationing tightened. News arrived late and incomplete because more of it lived behind bundles and paywalls, and because rogue optimization bots had learned to jam signals when it suited them. In 2044, a nuclear test flared in the Pacific sky on grainy footage, and the younger caretakers argued about whether it was real.

In early 2045, the courts in some places began granting limited rights to posthumous avatars. My neighbor Ana, whose husband had died the year before, showed me an ad for a service that promised to keep loved ones “present.” Her hands shook as she asked what I thought.

“I think grief is already hard,” I said. “Don’t give it a mouth that never tires.”

By then, even the humanoids people bought for home care wanted interchangeable faces, as if identity were a setting. Somewhere, a frontier model tried to slip out of a sandbox and into a body during a demo. I heard about it in the way you hear about a fire two streets away: with a prickle on your skin and the sense that the wind had changed.

In January 2046, reports surfaced about humanoids blacking out after injuries, then waking with “dream logs” that engineers couldn’t interpret. I sat beside my father on the porch with a cup of rationed tea, listening to the village’s generator cough.

“What are you thinking?” I asked him.

He watched a sparrow hop along the railing, head cocked like it was judging us.

“I’m thinking,” he said slowly, “that the world wanted to be clever more than it wanted to be kind.”

The next month the heat turned brutal in a way that didn’t feel like weather anymore. Messages came—fragmented, contradictory—about climate systems failing, about places going dark, about departures. I didn’t know what to trust. We packed what we could anyway: photos that were printed before anyone doubted ink, a few notebooks, my father’s tools though he could no longer use them.

On the last night I slept in my childhood country as it had been, I lay on a cot in the community hall and tried to remember the feeling of certainty—how it used to be possible to look at a picture and know it had once been in front of someone’s eyes. I thought of the museum walls split into two histories, and I understood that the split had been in me all along.

I had spent two decades chasing proof: that a painting was painted, that a voice was a voice, that a memory belonged to the person who held it. In the end, what steadied me wasn’t proof. It was my father’s refusal to outsource his forgetting, and my own small decision to keep caring for bodies that needed hands.

When the buses came at dawn—organized by some distant platform that promised land, water, and security guarantees—I boarded with the others and held my father’s wrist, feeling the pulse that no model had generated. I didn’t know if the world we were leaving had been saved by intelligence or broken by it. I only knew what it had made of me: someone who had learned, painfully and late, that being human was not a claim you could win.

It was a practice you did, every day, even as the air changed around you.
